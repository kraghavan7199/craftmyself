Training Across The Lifespan

The core principles of training remain the same, but their application should adapt as we age. Tailoring your approach ensures both safety and continued progress, whether you're a teen building a foundation or a master athlete preserving strength.

For Teenagers (Under 20)

The primary goal is building a solid technical foundation and work capacity.

  • Focus on Form: Emphasize mastering movement patterns over lifting maximal weight.
  • Avoid 1RM Testing: Use higher rep ranges (5-12) to build muscle and skill without the risks of maximal loads on a developing skeleton.
  • Fuel for Growth: Caloric needs are high. Ensure adequate protein and overall energy intake to support both training and natural growth.

For Older Adults (40+)

The focus shifts to preserving muscle mass, maintaining bone density, and managing recovery.

  • Heavy Lifting is Crucial: Resistance training is the single best defense against age-related sarcopenia (muscle loss) and osteoporosis.
  • Prioritize Recovery: Recovery capacity diminishes with age. You may need more rest days, longer warm-ups, and more proactive deloads.
  • Increase Protein: Research suggests older adults may need more protein (~1.2-1.6 g/kg) to stimulate muscle protein synthesis effectively.
  • Manage Volume: You can and should train hard, but you may not be able to tolerate the same weekly volume as a 20-year-old. Quality over quantity.
